## Different Types of Panel Formats

### **1. Dyad Panel (Two-Person Format) / 双人座谈**

**What it is:** A structured conversation between exactly two people - either two experts, or a moderator and one expert.

**Objective:**
- Deep, intimate exploration of a topic
- Build strong personal connections
- Allow for more detailed storytelling and nuanced discussion
- Reduce pressure on speakers who may be uncomfortable with larger panels

**Hosting differences:**
- Requires strong chemistry between the two participants
- Moderator (if present) must be highly skilled at drawing out conversation
- More vulnerable to one person dominating
- Higher risk if conversation doesn't flow naturally

**Participants:** 2 people total (either expert-to-expert or moderator + expert)

**Real examples:**
- **Fireside Chats** (most common dyad format): A moderator interviewing a CEO at a tech conference about their startup journey
- **GitLab's Customer Fireside Chats**: Informal conversations between GitLab team and key customers
- **Festival of Marketing**: Opening fireside chat with Lord Sugar that received 115 audience questions
- **Dyadic Interviews in research**: Healthcare researchers interviewing pairs of staff members who work together to understand shared experiences

---

### **2. Fireside Chat / 炉边谈话**

**What it is:** An informal yet structured conversation between a moderator and one or two guest speakers, designed to feel like an intimate dialogue.

**Objective:**
- Create personal, authentic connection between speaker and audience
- Extract stories and insights that wouldn't emerge in formal presentations
- Make it easier to secure high-profile speakers who don't want to prepare formal presentations
- Reduce speaker anxiety through conversational format

**Hosting differences:**
- Casual seating (comfortable armchairs, not podiums)
- No or minimal slides/PowerPoint
- Moderator must do extensive research beforehand
- Often includes audience Q&A section
- 45-90 minutes typical duration

**Participants:** 1 moderator + 1-2 experts

**Real examples:**
- SaaStr Conference: Jason Lemkin (VC and founder) conducting fireside chats with tech founders
- PCMA Convening Leaders in Vancouver replacing solo keynotes with fireside chats
- Marketing conferences with CMOs discussing digital marketing evolution
- Corporate town halls where CEOs address organizational change

---

### **3. Traditional Panel Discussion / 传统专家座谈**

**What it is:** 3-5 experts plus a moderator discussing a topic from multiple angles, typically on a stage.

**Objective:**
- Present diverse perspectives on a single topic
- Generate debate and dialogue between experts
- Provide comprehensive coverage of an issue
- Engage audience through variety of viewpoints

**Hosting differences:**
- Moderator keeps discussion flowing and balanced
- More formal than fireside chats
- Requires careful panelist selection for complementary/contrasting views
- 45-60 minutes typical duration
- Usually includes audience Q&A

**Participants:** 1 moderator + 3-5 panelists (3-4 is ideal)

**Real examples:**
- Academic conferences discussing research findings
- Industry forums debating digital vs. traditional marketing approaches
- Non-profit hosting panel on mental health in workplace with mental health experts, advocacy representatives, and HR professionals

---

### **4. Fishbowl Discussion / 鱼缸式座谈**

**What it is:** A small group (3-6 people) discusses in an inner circle while the larger group observes from an outer circle, with participants rotating in and out.

**Objective:**
- Make large group discussions more manageable and give focus to core conversation
- Practice active listening skills
- Bring transparency when public officials or decision-makers are in the fishbowl
- Encourage diverse participation over time

**Hosting differences:**
- Unique room setup: inner circle of 3-6 chairs, outer circle of observers
- Can have "open chair" for audience members to join inner circle
- Observers take notes but cannot speak
- Groups rotate every 8-15 minutes
- Requires strong facilitation to manage rotations

**Participants:** 3-6 in inner circle at any time, up to 50 total participants

**Real examples:**
- Public officials discussing policy decisions with community observing
- Educational settings discussing controversial topics like segregation cases
- Corporate workshops exploring workplace inclusion
- Research focus groups where different stakeholder groups take turns discussing

---

### **5. World Café / 世界咖啡馆式座谈**

**What it is:** Participants rotate between small tables (4-5 people) every 20-30 minutes, with one "table host" staying to summarize previous discussions, allowing ideas to cross-pollinate.

**Objective:**
- Enable exploration and discussion within large, heterogeneous groups
- Generate collective intelligence through cross-pollination of ideas
- Build on conversations progressively
- Foster intimate exchange despite large group size

**Hosting differences:**
- Café-like atmosphere with small round tables, refreshments, paper tablecloths for writing
- 3-4 rounds of 20-30 minute discussions
- Table host stays while others rotate
- Questions become progressively deeper with each round
- Final "harvest" session where insights are shared with full group
- Can accommodate 12-2,000 participants

**Participants:** 4-5 per table, scalable to hundreds of participants

**Real examples:**
- e.kundenservice Netz GmbH holding simultaneous World Cafés with 220 employees in Hamburg and Landshut to discuss organizational culture
- Community engagement on local projects
- Corporate strategy meetings enabling cross-level employee input
- Research projects with 45 participants at kickoff meetings

---

### **6. Debate Panel / 辩论式座谈**

**What it is:** Panelists argue opposing views while moderator keeps discussion structured, typically two sides presenting contrasting positions.

**Objective:**
- Sharpen understanding of different viewpoints
- Create engaging, dynamic content through respectful disagreement
- Help audience see multiple sides of controversial issues
- Generate energy and audience engagement

**Hosting differences:**
- Moderator must maintain balance and civility
- Clear structure with equal speaking time for each side
- May involve audience voting or polling
- Requires panelists comfortable with respectful disagreement

**Participants:** 2-4 panelists (usually equal number on each side) + moderator

**Real examples:**
- Marketing experts debating digital versus traditional approaches
- Policy debates on regulatory issues
- Investment strategies: traditional investor vs. crypto strategist
- Academic debates on theoretical approaches

---

### **7. Roundtable Discussion / 圆桌座谈**

**What it is:** Panelists are equals sharing experiences and solutions, like coaches comparing strategies, with no formal moderator dominance.

**Objective:**
- Peer-to-peer knowledge sharing
- Collaborative problem-solving
- Build community among practitioners
- More egalitarian than traditional panels

**Hosting differences:**
- All participants at equal level (no elevated expert)
- More free-flowing, less structured
- Moderator is more of a facilitator than controller
- Intimate setting, often literally around a round table
- 60-90 minutes typical

**Participants:** 4-8 peers + light-touch facilitator

**Real examples:**
- Coaching summits where coaches share client growth strategies
- Mastermind groups discussing business challenges
- Peer learning sessions among entrepreneurs
- Executive roundtables discussing industry trends

---

### **8. Audience-Driven Panel / 观众驱动座谈**

**What it is:** The audience determines topics through live voting, questions submitted in advance, or real-time polling.

**Objective:**
- Ensure relevance to audience interests
- Increase engagement and investment
- Adapt to what matters most to attendees
- Unpredictable and dynamic content

**Hosting differences:**
- Questions gathered ahead of time or in real-time, audience can "like" favorite questions
- Technology required (Slido, polling tools)
- Panelists must think quickly
- Less predictable than scripted panels

**Participants:** 3-5 panelists + moderator + active audience

**Real examples:**
- Festival of Marketing fireside chat with Lord Sugar receiving 115 audience questions with top question getting 73 upvotes
- Tech conferences using live Q&A platforms
- Webinars with crowdsourced questions

---

### **9. Presentation-Style Panel / 演讲式座谈**

**What it is:** Each panelist gets dedicated time to present their research/views before opening to discussion.

**Objective:**
- Share in-depth expertise from each panelist
- Ensure all viewpoints get full expression
- Work well when complex information needs clear presentation
- Combine benefits of presentations with panel discussion

**Hosting differences:**
- More structured and formal
- 5-10 minutes per panelist for initial presentation
- Q&A follows all presentations
- Requires strict time management
- Can feel less dynamic than conversational panels

**Participants:** 3-4 panelists + moderator + audience

**Real examples:**
- Academic research conferences where each panelist presents a paper
- Technical conferences presenting different approaches to same problem
- Scientific symposiums sharing research findings

## **Comparison: Panel Conference vs. Individual User Interviews**

Here's how panel conferences differ from traditional one-on-one TA (Target Audience) user interviews from a high-level perspective:

| Dimension | Panel Conference (座谈会) | Individual User Interview |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Group Dynamics** | Leverage interaction, debate, idea-building between participants | Pure one-on-one, no peer influence |
| **Depth vs. Breadth** | Broader coverage, multiple perspectives quickly | Deeper dive into individual experience |
| **Efficiency** | 6-10 perspectives in 2-3 hours | 1 perspective per hour |
| **Social Influence** | Groupthink and dominant voices possible | No social pressure, more honest about sensitive topics |
| **Spontaneity** | Ideas spark new ideas, organic discussion | Controlled, structured questioning |
| **Use Case** | Strategic direction, market positioning, trend identification | Detailed user journeys, personal pain points, usability issues |
| **Data Type** | Collective insights, consensus or debate | Individual narratives, specific behaviors |
| **Facilitation Complexity** | High - managing multiple personalities, keeping on track | Moderate - building rapport, asking good questions |

I hope this comprehensive overview helps you understand the different panel formats available for your marketing research! Each format serves different purposes, and choosing the right one depends on your objectives, participants, and desired outcomes.
