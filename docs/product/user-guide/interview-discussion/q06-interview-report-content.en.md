# What's in the Interview Report? Where to View It?

## Question Type
User Manual Question

---

## Quick Answer

**Interview report includes**:
- Interview Summary: Core findings from each persona
- Highlight Dialogue Snippets: As evidence
- Consensus and Divergence: Auto-identified
- User Profiles: 7-dimension detailed information
- Improvement Recommendations: Actionable suggestions

**View Location**:
- Interview project detail page → "View Report"
- Or Homepage → "My Projects" → Select project → "View Report"

---

## Interview Chat Report Structure

### 1. Executive Summary

**Content**:
- Research objective
- Number of interviewed personas
- Key findings (3-5 core insights)

**Example**:
```
Research Objective: Test sparkling coffee acceptance and pricing strategy
Interviewed Personas: 5 (price-sensitive, health-conscious, social trendsetter types)

Key Findings:
1. Users unwilling to take risks in "afternoon energy scenarios," recommend repositioning to morning exploration scenarios
2. 0-sugar concept is attractive, but need to address sweetener concerns
3. Price sensitivity point at ¥15-18, avoid exceeding ¥20
```

---

### 2. Individual Interview Summary (3000+ words per person)

**Structure**:

#### User Profile
- Basic information: Name, age, gender, occupation, city
- 7-dimension detailed information:
  - Demographics: Income, education, family
  - Psychological traits: Values, lifestyle
  - Behavioral traits: Consumption habits, decision logic
  - Pain points and needs: Real pain points, expectations
  - Technology acceptance: Attitude toward new products
  - Social relationships: Social circle influence
  - Geographic location: City, living environment

---

#### Core Findings

**Example**: Linda's interview summary
```
Core Findings:
1. Price sensitivity: ¥18 exceeds daily budget (usually buys ¥12 Americano)
2. Scenario limitation: Unwilling to risk trying new flavors in afternoon energy scenarios
3. Risk aversion: "If disappointed, my whole day's mood is ruined"
4. Trial scenarios: Morning or weekend willing to try, can bear disappointment risk

Key Insight:
- Surface: Expensive price
- Deep: Risk aversion psychology in afternoon energy scenarios
```

---

#### Highlight Dialogue Snippets

**Authentic dialogue as evidence**:

**Example**:
```
Q: Would you purchase ¥18 sparkling coffee?
A: Probably not.

Q: Why not?
A: Feels a bit expensive, and sparkling + coffee sounds weird.

Q: Weird? Are you worried about bad taste?
A: Yes, and at that afternoon time, I'm not in the mood to take risks.
   Coffee is for energy, not for trying new things. If disappointed, my whole day's mood is ruined.

Q: In what scenarios would you be willing to try new flavors?
A: Morning or weekend, when I can bear the risk of disappointment.
```

**Value**:
- Authentic dialogue, high credibility
- Can be used directly for reports and presentations
- Shows deep follow-up process

---

### 3. Cross-User Analysis

#### Commonalities (Consensus Areas)

**Auto-identify common viewpoints among multiple users**:

**Example**:
```
Consensus 1: 0-sugar concept is attractive
- 4 out of 5 mentioned "0-sugar is healthy"
- But all worried about sweetener safety

Consensus 2: Packaging appearance is important
- 3 out of 5 mentioned "if looks good will post on Xiaohongshu"
- Social value influences purchase decisions

Consensus 3: Price sensitivity point ¥15-18
- 4 out of 5 indicated "¥18 acceptable but near upper limit"
- Exceeding ¥20 causes "hesitation"
```

---

#### Differences (Divergence Areas)

**Opposing viewpoints among different users**:

**Example**:
```
Divergence 1: Price acceptance
- Price-sensitive: ¥18 too expensive, budget ¥12 and under
- Quality-first: ¥18 acceptable, key is quality
- Social trendsetter: Don't care about price, photo-worthy is enough

Divergence 2: Trial scenarios
- Linda: Unwilling to risk in afternoon energy scenarios
- Chloe: Willing to try new flavors anytime

Divergence 3: Focus points
- Emma: Focus on ingredients and health
- Chloe: Focus on appearance and social value
- Linda: Focus on price and value
```

---

#### User Segments

**Auto-clustering based on interviews**:

**Example**:
```
Segment 1: Price-Sensitive Type (40%)
- Representatives: Linda, Sarah
- Characteristics: Limited budget, ¥12-15 range
- Decision factor: Value first

Segment 2: Health-Conscious Type (40%)
- Representatives: Emma, Mia
- Characteristics: Focus on ingredients and health
- Decision factor: 0-sugar, natural, no additives

Segment 3: Social Trendsetter Type (20%)
- Representatives: Chloe
- Characteristics: Focus on appearance and novelty
- Decision factor: Photo-worthy, topical
```

---

### 4. Actionable Improvement Recommendations

**Specific action plans based on insights**:

**Example**:
```
Recommendation 1: Adjust scenario positioning
- Problem: Users unwilling to risk in afternoon energy scenarios
- Action: Position as "morning energy scenario" or "weekend exploration scenario"
- Expected outcome: Lower trial barrier, increase conversion rate

Recommendation 2: Address sweetener concerns
- Problem: Users worried about sweetener safety
- Action: Label "natural sweetness, no additives," display ingredients list
- Expected outcome: Increase trust among health-conscious users

Recommendation 3: Optimize packaging design
- Problem: Social trendsetter users focus on appearance
- Action: Design high-appearance packaging suitable for photo sharing
- Expected outcome: Increase social spread, boost purchase intent

Recommendation 4: Pricing strategy
- Problem: ¥18 near users' psychological upper limit
- Action: Price at ¥15-16, or launch ¥12 small-size option
- Expected outcome: Cover more price-sensitive users
```

---

### 5. Appendix

**Complete interview records**:
- Complete dialogue records for each persona
- Interview question list
- Interview duration and date

## Discussion Chat Report Structure

### 1. Discussion Summary

**Content**:
- Discussion topic
- Participating personas (3-8 people)
- Discussion duration
- Core viewpoint summary

---

### 2. Viewpoint Collision Key Moments

**Highlight dialogue snippets**:

**Example**:
```
【Controversy: Subscription vs One-Time Purchase】

Small Business A: I prefer monthly payment, high flexibility
Mid-Size Business B: We prefer annual payment, has discount

AI Moderator: Why do your preferences differ so much?

Small Business A: Our cash flow is tight, monthly payment doesn't pressure finances
Mid-Size Business B: We have budget planning, annual payment more cost-effective, reduces procurement frequency

(Key insight: Company size determines payment preference)
```

---

### 3. Consensus and Divergence List

**Auto-identify**:

#### Consensus Areas
```
Consensus 1: Powerful features are core value
- All 6 people agree
- But definition of "powerful" differs (small businesses focus on ease of use, mid-size businesses focus on scalability)

Consensus 2: Trial period is important
- All 6 people mentioned "need trial before deciding"
- Recommend extending trial period to 30 days
```

---

#### Divergence Areas
```
Divergence 1: Payment method preference
- Small businesses (3 people): Prefer monthly payment
- Mid-size businesses (3 people): Prefer annual payment

Divergence 2: Price sensitivity
- Small businesses: Extremely price-sensitive
- Mid-size businesses: More concerned with ROI, don't care about price
```

---

#### Controversy Points (Most Heated Discussion)
```
Controversy: "Can AI personal trainer replace human coach?"
- Supporters (2 people): AI already sufficient, human too expensive
- Opponents (3 people): AI doesn't understand my body, don't trust

(Key insight: Users don't reject AI, but worry "AI doesn't understand me")
```

---

### 4. Viewpoint Evolution Tracking

**Before vs after discussion**:

**Example**:
```
AI Persona A's viewpoint evolution:
- Before discussion: "Sparkling coffee sounds weird, won't buy"
- During discussion: Heard others say "actually pretty good"
- After discussion: "Hearing you say that, I might try it"

(Key finding: Social discussion can change viewpoints, word-of-mouth is important)
```

---

### 5. Group Dynamics Analysis

**Speaking statistics**:
- Each persona's speaking count
- Speaking duration percentage
- Leader vs silent

**Influence analysis**:
- Whose viewpoints most persuasive?
- Who was convinced?
- Key persuasion moments

---

### 6. Actionable Improvement Recommendations

**Similar to Interview**, provide action plans based on discussion findings.

## Common Questions

### Q1: What if report generation fails?

**Common causes**:
- Interview incomplete
- System busy

**Solutions**:
- Refresh page and retry
- Contact customer service

---

### Q2: Can reports be edited?

**Cannot directly edit system-generated reports**.

**But can**:
- Export as Word format
- Manually edit then share

---

### Q3: Do reports expire?

**No**. All historical reports are saved.

**Storage limits**:
- Free version: Maximum 10 reports
- Paid version: Unlimited

---

### Q4: How to find historical reports?

**Steps**:
1. Homepage → "Report Center"
2. Use search or filters
3. Sort by date, project name

## Best Practices

### Recommendation 1: Combine Highlight Dialogue Snippets for Reporting

**Don't just say**: "Users think it's expensive"

**Say**:
- "User Linda said: '¥18 exceeds my daily budget'"
- Quote authentic dialogue, higher credibility

---

### Recommendation 2: Focus on "Actionable Recommendations"

**Most valuable part of report**:
- Actionable improvement recommendations
- Specific action plans based on insights
- Expected outcome assessment

---

### Recommendation 3: Export PDF for Archiving

**Important reports recommended**:
- Export PDF format
- Archive backup
- Convenient for long-term tracking

---

## Final Takeaway

> "Report is not the endpoint, it's the starting point of action.
> Key is 'actionable recommendations,' not 'insights' themselves."

**Remember**:
- ✅ Report includes: Summary, dialogue snippets, consensus/divergence, improvement recommendations
- ✅ View location: Project detail page → "View Report"
- ✅ Most valuable: Actionable improvement recommendations
