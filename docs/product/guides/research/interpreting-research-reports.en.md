# How to interpret AI Research-generated reports? Which parts are most important?

## Question Type
User Manual Question

---

## Quick Answer

**The 3 Most Important Parts**:
1. **Executive Summary**: Core findings (3-5 insights)
2. **Actionable Recommendations**: Specific action plans
3. **Highlight Conversation Excerpts**: Evidence for insights

**Reading Order**:
1. First read executive summary (5 minutes) → Understand core findings
2. Then read actionable recommendations (5 minutes) → Know what to do
3. When in doubt, read detailed interviews (30 minutes) → Understand "why"

---

## Complete Report Structure Interpretation

### Part 1: Executive Summary (Most Important)

**Location**: First page of report

**Content**:
- Research objectives
- Participant personas (count and types)
- Core findings (3-5 insights)

---

#### Example: Sparkling Coffee New Product Test

```
Executive Summary

Research Objective:
Test sparkling coffee new product acceptance and pricing strategy

Interview Personas: 5 people
- 2 price-sensitive types (Linda, Sarah)
- 2 health-anxious types (Emma, Mia)
- 1 social early-adopter (Chloe)

Core Findings:

1. ⚠️ Unwilling to risk in afternoon energy-boost scenario
   Users unwilling to try new products during afternoon work, worry disappointment affects state.
   Recommend adjusting to breakfast or weekend scenario.

2. ✅ Zero-sugar concept attractive, but need to address sweetener concerns
   Users interested in zero-sugar, but worry about sweetener safety.
   Recommend labeling "natural sweetness, no additives".

3. 💰 Price sensitivity point at ¥15-18
   ¥18 near users' psychological upper limit, ¥20 causes hesitation.
   Recommend pricing ¥15-16.

4. 📸 Packaging appearance affects social sharing
   Social early-adopter users care about packaging design, attractive ones get photographed and shared.
   Recommend designing high-appearance packaging.

5. 🔀 Significant differences between user groups
   Price-sensitive types focus on value, health-anxious types focus on ingredients,
   social early-adopters focus on appearance. Need segmented marketing.
```

---

#### How to Interpret Executive Summary

**Understand Core Findings**:
- ⚠️ Warning: Risks to note
- ✅ Opportunity: Advantages to leverage
- 💰 Key Data: Important quantitative indicators
- 📸 User Insights: Deep psychology and motivations
- 🔀 Difference Analysis: Variations between groups

**Quick Judgment**:
- Is research direction correct?
- Any unexpected findings?
- Which findings most important?

---

### Part 2: Actionable Recommendations (Most Important)

**Location**: Report end or separate section

**Content**:
- Specific action plans based on insights
- Expected effects
- Priorities

---

#### Example: Sparkling Coffee Actionable Recommendations

```
Actionable Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Adjust Scenario Positioning (High Priority)
Problem: Users unwilling to risk in afternoon energy-boost scenario
Action:
- Position as "breakfast vitality time" or "weekend trial scenario"
- Marketing copy:
  "First cup in the morning, energize your whole day"

Expected Effect:
- Lower trial barrier
- Increase conversion rate 15-20%

Recommendation 2: Address Sweetener Concerns (High Priority)
Problem: Users worry about sweetener safety
Action:
- Prominently label "natural sweetness, no additives" on packaging
- Display ingredient list
- Provide authoritative certification

Expected Effect:
- Increase health-anxious user trust
- Reduce purchase hesitation

Recommendation 3: Optimize Packaging Design (Medium Priority)
Problem: Social early-adopter users care about appearance
Action:
- Design high-appearance packaging (photo-friendly)
- Consider limited editions/collaborations
- Encourage user social sharing

Expected Effect:
- Increase social spread
- Increase purchase intent 10-15%

Recommendation 4: Pricing Strategy (High Priority)
Problem: ¥18 near users' psychological upper limit
Action:
- Price at ¥15-16
- Or launch ¥12 small size (lower trial barrier)

Expected Effect:
- Cover more price-sensitive users
- Increase trial rate

Recommendation 5: Segmented Marketing (Medium Priority)
Problem: Different users focus on different aspects
Action:
- Price-sensitive types: Emphasize value (¥16, zero-sugar health)
- Health-anxious types: Emphasize ingredient safety (natural, no additives)
- Social early-adopters: Emphasize appearance (photo-worthy, shareable)

Expected Effect:
- Increase conversion rates across different segments
```

---

#### How to Interpret Actionable Recommendations

**Understand Priorities**:
- High priority: Execute immediately, major impact
- Medium priority: Execute when resources allow
- Low priority: Selectively execute

**Evaluate Feasibility**:
- Can this recommendation be done?
- How many resources needed?
- Are expected effects reasonable?

**Create Action Plan**:
- Select 2-3 high-priority recommendations
- Assign responsible persons and timeline
- Track execution results

### Part 3: Individual Interview Details (Read for Deep Understanding)

**Location**: Middle of report

**Content**:
- Detailed interview records for each persona
- User profiles
- Highlight conversation excerpts

---

#### Example: Linda's Interview Details

```
Linda (28, Product Manager, Shanghai)

User Profile:
- Income: 15-20K
- Lifestyle: Busy on weekdays, efficiency-focused
- Consumption characteristics: Price-sensitive, rational decisions
- Pain points: Time-constrained, limited budget

Core Findings:
1. Unwilling to risk in afternoon energy-boost scenario
2. Price sensitivity point: ¥12-15
3. Need to see user reviews before purchasing

Highlight Conversation Excerpts:

Q: Would you buy ¥18 sparkling coffee?
A: Probably not, a bit expensive.

Q: Why do you think it's expensive?
A: I usually buy ¥12 Americano, ¥18 exceeds my budget.

Q: If it's ¥12, would you buy?
A: Still no, mainly sparkling + coffee sounds weird.

Q: Weird? What are you worried about?
A: Worried the taste won't be good. And at that afternoon time,
   I'm not in the mood to take risks. Coffee is for energy boost, not for trying new things.
   If disappointed, my whole day's state is ruined.

Q: In what scenario would you be willing to try?
A: Morning or weekend, then I can afford the risk of disappointment.

Key Insight:
- Surface: Expensive
- Deep: Risk aversion psychology in afternoon energy-boost scenario
- Recommendation: Adjust to breakfast or weekend scenario
```

---

#### When to Read Detailed Interviews

**Situation 1: Don't Understand Insight**
- Executive summary says "unwilling to risk in afternoon"
- Don't understand why?
- Read detailed interview to understand underlying logic

**Situation 2: Need to Persuade Others**
- When reporting to boss
- Use highlight conversation excerpts as evidence
- "User Linda said: 'No mood to risk in afternoon'"

**Situation 3: Deeply Optimize Product**
- Want to understand users' complete thought process
- Path from awareness → hesitation → decision
- Discover more optimization opportunities

---

### Part 4: Cross-user Analysis (Read to Understand Differences)

**Location**: Middle or back of report

**Content**:
- Common points (consensus)
- Difference points (disagreements)
- User segmentation

---

#### Example: Cross-user Analysis

```
Cross-user Analysis

Consensus Areas:
1. Zero-sugar concept attractive (5/5 people mentioned)
2. Packaging appearance important (3/5 people mentioned)
3. Need to see ingredient list (4/5 people mentioned)

Disagreement Areas:
1. Price acceptance
   - Price-sensitive types (Linda, Sarah): Within ¥15
   - Quality-first types (Emma, Mia): ¥20 acceptable
   - Social early-adopter (Chloe): Don't care about price

2. Purchase scenario
   - Linda: Breakfast or weekend
   - Emma: Anytime
   - Chloe: Social occasions

3. Core appeal
   - Price-sensitive types: Value
   - Health-anxious types: Ingredient safety
   - Social early-adopters: Appearance and novelty

User Segmentation:
Segment 1: Price-sensitive types (40%)
- Focus on value
- Budget ¥12-15
- Need to see user reviews

Segment 2: Health-anxious types (40%)
- Focus on ingredients and health
- Willing to pay more for zero-sugar
- Need authoritative certification

Segment 3: Social early-adopters (20%)
- Focus on appearance and novelty
- Not price-sensitive
- Willing to share on social media
```

---

#### How to Use Cross-user Analysis

**Understand Consensus**:
- Points all users agree on
- Use as core selling points
- Communicate first

**Understand Disagreements**:
- Differentiated needs of different users
- Segmented marketing
- Avoid "one-size-fits-all"

**User Segmentation**:
- Identify core target users
- Design differentiated strategies
- Prioritize

## Interpretation Tips

### Tip 1: Read Executive Summary and Recommendations First

**5-minute Quick Understanding**:
- What are core findings?
- What's recommended to do?
- Any unexpected findings?

**Don't Start with Detailed Interviews**:
- Information overload
- Miss key points

---

### Tip 2: Use Highlight Conversations to Persuade Others

**Don't Just Say**: "Users think it's expensive"

**Say Instead**:
> "User Linda said: '¥18 exceeds my daily budget,
> I usually buy ¥12 Americano.'
> This reflects real feelings of price-sensitive users."

**Original Conversations**:
- More persuasive
- Authentic and credible
- Easy to understand

---

### Tip 3: Focus on "Why" Rather Than "What"

**Not Important**: "40% of users think it's expensive"

**Important**: "Why do users think it's expensive?
          - Price-sensitive types: Exceeds budget
          - Health-anxious types: Not sure if worth it
          - Solution: Show value, address concerns"

**Deep Insights > Surface Numbers**

---

### Tip 4: Identify Unexpected Findings

**Expected Findings**:
- "Users care about price" (common)

**Unexpected Findings**:
- "Unwilling to risk in afternoon energy-boost scenario" (insight)
- This is real value

**Prioritize Unexpected Findings**:
- Challenge assumptions
- Discover new opportunities
- Avoid strategic mistakes

## Common Misreadings

### Misreading 1: Over-focus on Percentages

❌ **Wrong**:
"40% of users like it, so product doesn't work"

✅ **Correct**:
"2 out of 5 like it, 3 don't.
 Those who like are health-anxious types, those who don't are price-sensitive types.
 Recommend marketing to health-anxious types, price ¥15-16 to cover more people."

**Reason**:
- Small sample (5-10 people), percentages meaningless
- What matters is "why" and "what to do"

---

### Misreading 2: Ignore Actionable Recommendations

❌ **Wrong**:
Only read insights, not recommendations

✅ **Correct**:
Read insights + recommendations together, know what to do

---

### Misreading 3: Accept All Recommendations Wholesale

❌ **Wrong**:
Do whatever AI says

✅ **Correct**:
- Evaluate recommendation feasibility
- Consider actual situation
- Select 2-3 high-priority ones to execute

## Best Practices

### Recommendation 1: Interpret as Team

**Workflow**:
1. Research lead reads report first
2. Extract core findings
3. Team meeting discussion
4. Jointly create action plan

---

### Recommendation 2: Annotate Key Points

**On Report**:
- Highlight core findings
- Mark priorities
- Record questions

**For**:
- Future review
- Present to others
- Track execution

---

### Recommendation 3: Continuous Tracking

**Don't**:
- End after reading report

**Do**:
- Execute recommendations
- Track results
- Validate if insights accurate

---

## Bottom Line

> "Interpret report: First read executive summary and recommendations (10 minutes),
> if questions read detailed interviews (30 minutes).
> Key is taking action based on insights, not just reading report."

**Remember**:
- ✅ Most important: Executive summary + Actionable recommendations
- ✅ Deep understanding: Read detailed interviews
- ✅ Persuade with conversations: Quote highlight excerpts
- ✅ Focus on "why": Not just "what"
- ✅ Take action: Execute based on recommendations
